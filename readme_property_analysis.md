# 🏠 Property Price Register (PPR) Analysis
### Visual Insights from Irish Housing Market Trends

---

## 📌 Project Overview

This project explores major patterns in the Irish **Property Price Register (PPR)** dataset, focusing on:

✨ Monthly property price movements across counties  
✨ VAT reporting inconsistencies in new dwellings  
✨ Long-term transaction trends by property type  

The goal is to identify:

- regional housing market differences
- structural supply challenges
- data quality issues affecting research reliability

This analysis helps provide a clearer understanding of how Ireland’s residential property market has evolved over time.

---

# 📈 1. Average Monthly Property Prices Comparison

## 🏷 Graph Title
### **Average Monthly Property Prices Comparison**

## 📍 Counties Analysed
🔹 Dublin  
🔹 Tipperary  
🔹 Westmeath  

## 🎯 Purpose
This visual compares the **average monthly residential property prices** across three counties with very different market characteristics.

It also highlights two important timeline markers:

🟦 **2012 Recovery** → Post-financial-crisis recovery period  
🟦 **2025 Marker** → Most recent point in the dataset

---

## 🔍 Key Findings

### 🏙 Dublin consistently dominates prices
Dublin shows significantly higher average prices throughout the entire period compared to Tipperary and Westmeath.

This reflects:

- stronger housing demand
- higher population concentration
- greater investor activity
- stronger employment opportunities
- persistent supply constraints

📌 By 2025, Dublin prices rise close to **€900,000** in some monthly observations.

---

### 🌿 Tipperary remains the most affordable market
Tipperary shows the lowest average prices across the full timeline.

Price growth exists, but it is gradual and far less volatile than Dublin.

This suggests:

- lower speculative pressure
- lower urban demand intensity
- more stable regional pricing

---

### 📊 Westmeath shows stronger growth than Tipperary
Westmeath begins close to Tipperary but gradually separates over time.

Its upward movement suggests stronger regional demand and improved market activity relative to smaller counties.

---

### 🚀 Strong upward acceleration after 2012
All three counties show clearer price growth after the 2012 recovery marker.

This supports the broader national recovery trend following the housing crash and financial crisis.

---

### ⚠ Increased volatility after 2018
Price fluctuations become much sharper, especially in Dublin.

Possible reasons include:

- lower transaction volumes in some months
- premium property transactions affecting averages
- post-COVID market disruptions
- inflation and supply-side pressure

📌 This is why **median price analysis** is often preferred over simple averages in academic work.

---

## ✅ Conclusion
The Irish housing market is highly uneven geographically.

Dublin behaves as a high-pressure market, while counties like Tipperary remain relatively stable and affordable.

Westmeath sits between these two extremes, showing moderate but meaningful growth.

# 🧾 2. New Dwellings with VAT Exclusive = No (by County)

## 🏷 Graph Title
### **New Dwellings with VAT Exclusive = No (by County)**

## 🎯 Purpose
This graph investigates a known **data quality issue** in the PPR dataset.

For genuinely new dwellings, VAT treatment should normally be carefully recorded. However, many properties are labelled:

- **Description = New Dwelling**
- **VAT Exclusive = No**

This creates a potential reporting inconsistency worth investigating.

---

## 🔍 Key Findings

### 🏢 Dublin is a major outlier
Dublin has by far the highest number of records with this issue, exceeding **700 observations**.

This is dramatically higher than all other counties.

Possible reasons include:

- significantly higher construction volume
- more frequent reporting inconsistencies
- administrative recording variation
- larger concentration of apartment developments

---

### 🌆 Cork and Galway also show elevated counts
After Dublin, counties such as Cork and Galway show the next highest counts.

These are also major urban centres with stronger development activity.

This suggests the issue is more common where new-build activity is highest.

---

### 🌾 Smaller counties show much lower counts
Counties like Monaghan, Cavan, and Longford have very few such records.

This may reflect:

- lower development intensity
- fewer apartment developments
- smaller transaction volumes

---

### 🎯 The issue is not random
The pattern is clearly concentrated rather than evenly distributed.

This suggests the problem is likely structural rather than accidental.

It may be linked to how developers, solicitors, or reporting systems classify VAT treatment.

---

## ✅ Conclusion
VAT-related inconsistencies are strongly concentrated in high-activity counties, especially Dublin.

This matters because incorrect VAT labels can distort analysis of:

- new-build supply
- affordability studies
- taxation research
- housing policy evaluation

📌 This confirms that **data cleaning is essential** before drawing conclusions from PPR new dwelling records.

# 📉 3. Yearly Property Transactions by Property Type

## 🏷 Graph Title
### **Yearly Property Transactions by Property Type**

## 🏠 Property Types Compared
🔹 New Dwelling house / Apartment  
🔹 Second-Hand Dwelling house / Apartment

## 🎯 Purpose
This graph compares annual transaction volumes between new and second-hand residential properties.

A vertical marker is added at **2020** to highlight the COVID period and its market impact.

---

## 🔍 Key Findings

### 🔁 Second-hand dwellings dominate the market
Throughout the full period, second-hand sales are substantially higher than new dwelling sales.

This indicates that Ireland’s housing market is primarily driven by existing housing stock rather than newly built supply.

---

### 📈 Strong growth from 2012 onward
Both categories show strong expansion after 2012.

This reflects:

- post-crash recovery
- improving mortgage availability
- stronger consumer confidence
- increasing housing demand

---

### 🏗 New dwelling supply remains structurally limited
Even during strong years, new dwelling transactions remain much lower.

This highlights a long-term supply problem where construction struggles to keep pace with demand.

This supports wider national discussions around housing shortages.

---

### 🦠 COVID shock visible in 2020
Both categories show a noticeable decline in 2020.

This is likely associated with:

- lockdown restrictions
- delayed transactions
- construction slowdowns
- uncertainty in the housing market

---

### 🔄 Recovery after 2020 is strong
Following the temporary decline, both property types recover quickly.

Second-hand transactions rebound particularly strongly, while new dwellings improve more gradually.

This suggests demand remained strong even when supply constraints persisted.

---

## ✅ Conclusion
Ireland’s housing market remains heavily dependent on second-hand transactions because new housing supply is insufficient.

The COVID disruption was temporary, but the structural supply imbalance remains a long-term issue.

This finding is especially important for understanding affordability pressures and housing policy challenges.

# 📚 Final Project Summary

Across all three visuals, the analysis highlights three major themes:

## 🔑 Core Findings

### 1. Strong regional inequality in house prices
Dublin remains structurally different from smaller counties, with significantly higher prices and stronger volatility.

### 2. Data quality concerns in VAT reporting
VAT inconsistencies are concentrated in counties with high development activity, especially Dublin.

### 3. Persistent dependence on second-hand housing
Ireland’s housing market remains heavily reliant on existing housing stock because new supply remains insufficient.

---

# 🎓 Final Research Insight

Both **market structure** and **data quality** must be carefully considered when analysing Irish housing trends using the PPR dataset.

Without proper cleaning and interpretation, misleading conclusions can easily be drawn from transaction-level housing data.
